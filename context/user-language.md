## Situation-language distinctions

These distinctions summarize the available language without claiming that the wording came from env users.

### Intention versus scrolling

The reader already intends to study. Natural language centers on `I need to study`, `I sit down to study`, `I end up on my phone`, and `I start scrolling` rather than on motivation, discipline, or addiction labels.

### Necessary phone versus distracting phone

The phone may be needed for notes, class messages, authentication, a calculator, recorded lectures, or a school app. Language should preserve both sides: `I need my phone for class` and `I get distracted` can be true at the same time. Do not simplify this into `the phone is the problem`.

### Sitting versus beginning

The recognizable contrast is between visible preparation—`I sat down`, `the notes are open`, `the timer is running`—and literal contact with the task—`write the first sentence`, `answer the first question`, `read the first paragraph`, or `continue the unfinished line`.

The task-action examples are editorial translations from legacy method materials, not recorded user quotations. Use them to make a scene concrete, not as evidence that users speak in those exact phrases.

### Reusing ordinary defaults

For recurring environment choices, prefer ordinary language such as `use the same timer`, `keep the sound I already chose`, or `start with the apps I need available`. Avoid internal labels such as `setup-choice fatigue`, `decision load`, `if-then condition`, `cue`, `trigger`, or `task action` in visible copy.

## Usage rule

- Do not present these lines as testimonials or env-user quotes.
- Do not copy a desk-research sentence into new content merely because it sounds natural.
- Use the material to understand situations and vocabulary only after checking fit with the active message.
- Preserve the difference between an unattributed quoted expression and an editorially written task example; neither is verified env-user language.
- Prefer reconstructing fresh, natural U.S. English from the underlying situation over lightly rewriting a stored expression.
- Add exact source, context, and confidence when the original material becomes available.
