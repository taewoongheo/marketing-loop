# Copywriting: denzel v1

## 1. Scope and evidence

This file owns how a selected LIFT CODE message is expressed in the `denzel` slideshow through language: slide-copy roles, progression, wording, voice, emphasis, CTA, title, and caption.

- Message meaning and claim limits come from the selected message version.
- Product truth comes from `context/product.md`.
- Reusable strength-training knowledge and provenance come from `context/expertise.md`.
- Each self-contained content project owns slide count, geometry, typography, image crop, and editable layers.
- `context/imagery.md` separately owns current account-wide semantic visualization, art direction, within-image composition, runtime request constraints, and generation/selection policy.
- `renderer/slideshow/formats/denzel/references/post-01/` through `post-05/` contain five user-designated posts in slide order. The first four have seven slides; `post-05` has eight.
- All five reference posts use one hook slide, a numbered body series, and the same low-information closing treatment. Body length and syntax vary.
- Reference slide counts describe source evidence, not a required project slide count.
- References are evidence of technique only. Never copy their topic, wording, examples, signature, or distinctive subjects.

Domain knowledge is global rather than Denzel-specific. This format selects only the expertise relevant to its approved message and decides how to express it; it does not own a separate content pool. Do not work around missing expertise by importing ad-hoc fitness tips directly into copy.

## 2. Slide flow

### Hook slide

Open with a useful, counterintuitive, or identity-relevant strength-training statement, then qualify it with a personal relationship or a grounded training perspective when that earns attention without pretending expertise.

```text
[reader-facing strength, progression, or avoided-error stake]
[optional lived relationship, former assumption, or specific training context]
```

- Make the main topic understandable without the qualifier.
- Lead with the lifter's stake: uncertainty before a Set, stalled progression, repeated bookkeeping, wasted effort, or the tension between aggression and control.
- Use the qualifier to establish relevance, not authority, product performance, or a guaranteed result.
- Promise a useful perspective or practical route, never guaranteed strength, hypertrophy, confidence, or progression.
- Across contents, vary hook architecture among useful outcomes, overlooked problems, contrasts, questions, personal admissions, and identity statements. Do not default to an `N ways` promise merely because body slides are numbered.
- Match hook grammar to the body. A methods hook must deliver methods; an explanatory claim must receive an explanation; an identity hook must resolve into a usable training principle.
- Do not use `secret`, `hack`, `crack the code`, hacker metaphors, or unsupported `science says` authority.

### Body slides

Use numbered body slides to develop distinct facets under one hook promise. Every facet must support the selected LIFT CODE message rather than drift into generic exercise selection, motivation, form correction, or physiology trivia.

When a numbered hook promises `N` methods and the project includes a dedicated CTA slide, count the CTA as the final method only when it genuinely performs one of the promised methods.

Before drafting, make an internal claim-to-owner map. Trace each factual strength-training assertion to a bounded entry in `context/expertise.md`, each product statement to `context/product.md`, and each audience expression to `context/user-language.md`. Message logic may organize those facts but cannot substitute for missing evidence. A slide may use more than one compatible expertise entry, and one entry may support several slides when the copy develops different implications; never manufacture artificial variety by adding unrelated advice.

1. The first body slide makes the hook concrete through one recognizable decision, behavior, or misconception.
2. The remaining body slides add distinct, parallel facets of the same promise.
3. The sequence may escalate from raw force toward controlled progression when the selected message supports it, but no fixed escalation is mandatory.
4. Every slide must advance the argument instead of restating “track your workouts” or “use progressive overload.”

Each body slide may use:

```text
[N]. [concise point title]

[recognizable training decision or correction].
[brief reason, constraint, or consequence].

[optional controlled conclusion].
```

The title may be an imperative, noun phrase, contrast, or short principle. Vary paragraph shape and sentence length. Select slide count from approved copy, reference evidence, and progression needs rather than forcing one reference count.

### CTA slide

Design a dedicated CTA slide that completes the selected training reframe and invites one relevant next action.

- State the useful principle before the CTA.
- Use an action that follows naturally from the content, such as saving the routine or following for future training decisions.
- Keep CTA reading length and density comparable to body slides.
- Resolve on the lifter using the decision or returning for another relevant one rather than introducing a separate promise.

## 3. Writing

### Reusable sentence patterns

Use these as flexible structures, not fixed wording:

```text
[aggressive instinct] gets you into the set.
[controlled system] decides what the next set earns.
```

```text
[common progression assumption].
[constraint or overlooked input].
[controlled replacement rule].
```

```text
The number is not trustworthy because an app chose it.
It becomes judgeable when you can see what changed and why.
```

```text
[observable performance] + [real loading constraint] → [next action]
```

Use only the parts needed. Do not repeat brand slogans across every slide.

### Voice, rhythm, and emphasis

- Use idiomatic conversational U.S. English and direct `you`.
- Sound masculine, controlled, exact, and physically grounded rather than loud, theatrical, clinical, or corporate.
- Use aggression to describe effort and ambition; use restraint to describe progression decisions.
- Prefer concrete training nouns and verbs: load, plate, rep, Set, RIR, bar, machine increment, maintain, earn, record, perform.
- Avoid vague `optimize`, `unlock`, `level up`, `beast mode`, `alpha`, `dominate`, and `no excuses` language.
- Do not shame a lifter for uncertainty, lower numbers, maintenance, deload, or a changed actual Set.
- Use short decisive sentences for the correction and slightly longer sentences only where a constraint or reason must be explained.
- Keep one main claim per sentence and one argument per slide.
- Use a small number of emphasized phrases carrying the decisive contrast or conclusion.
- Let the headline carry the claim; do not summarize the same claim again at the end.
- Run a separate idiomatic U.S.-English pass before showing copy.
- Preserve terminology within one content. Do not switch casually among routine, Workout, Program, session, target, recommendation, and prescription when they mean different things.
## 4. CTA and caption

The references do not contain a CTA. The dedicated CTA is an account adaptation, not a reference-derived rule.

- Keep the CTA centered on the selected belief shift rather than introducing a new subject.
- Provide a separate concise post title with every final copy. The title is not part of the caption.
- Compose the caption as one short description sentence followed by a small set of relevant hashtags.
- Let the caption extend the reframe rather than recap the slides or repeat the CTA.
- Hashtags are publication metadata, not performance evidence.
