# Copywriting: nomtzzz v1

## 1. Scope and evidence

This file owns how a selected LIFT CODE message is expressed through language in the `nomtzzz` slideshow format: slide roles, explanatory progression, wording, emphasis, information density, CTA, title, and caption.

- Message meaning and claim limits come from the selected message version.
- Product truth comes from `context/product.md`.
- Reusable strength-training knowledge, provenance, evidence status, scope, and exceptions come from `context/expertise.md` and its linked topic owners.
- Each native slideshow project owns exact slide count, canvas, geometry, typography, colors, character or figure assets, and editable layers.
- `context/imagery.md` owns the account-wide art direction and image-generation or selection policy. This copywriting file does not select or authorize a production character.
- `renderer/slideshow/formats/nomtzzz/references/post-01/1.png` through `6.png` and `post-02/1.png` through `6.png` contain two user-designated six-slide posts in exact order.
- Both posts progress from an attention-setting first slide through evidence or explanation, practical implications, and a final takeaway. Their internal roles differ, so six slides are recurring evidence rather than a mandatory template.
- The source-viewer search bar, black surround, navigation arrows, page dots, and mute control are capture chrome, not format grammar.
- The references use different recognizable anime characters across posts. The characters function as genre and attention cues rather than one stable narrator, expert, user, or testimonial.
- The character artwork is visual execution evidence, not copy authority or reusable production material. Never copy its identity, pose, clothing, artwork, or franchise associations, and never treat a character as evidence for a scientific claim.
- References are evidence of technique only. Never copy their topics, wording, prescriptions, citations, screenshots, scientific claims, errors, or distinctive expressions.

This format explains one bounded training decision through evidence-led progression at a user-controlled reading pace. It does not own scientific facts. If a required claim is missing from the global expertise owner, research and admit the bounded knowledge with provenance before drafting rather than borrowing a reference claim or citation.

## 2. Recurring progression

Use only the roles needed to fulfill the approved message. The observed posts support a six-part arc, but a project may combine, split, or omit roles when the argument remains complete.

### 1. Hook or topic cover

Open with either a specific training question or a bounded contrast that creates a reason to continue.

```text
[specific training stake]
[unexpected but supportable contrast]
```

- Make the subject understandable on the first slide.
- Lead with the lifter's decision, cost, or misconception rather than a generic educational-series label.
- A curiosity gap must reveal enough context to remain truthful; do not hide the relevant condition merely to make the claim sound larger.
- Do not use `science-based`, `research proves`, or a technical acronym as decoration.
- Do not promise guaranteed performance, injury prevention, hypertrophy, strength, or recommendation accuracy.

### 2. Evidence or practical reframe

The second step may establish the evidence basis or replace the opening assumption with a more useful interpretation.

For an evidence slide:

```text
[bounded finding]
[condition, comparison, or limitation]
Evidence:
[traceable source identification]
```

For a reframe slide:

```text
[common assumption or incomplete goal]
[what the decision must accomplish instead]
```

- Choose the role that best earns the next explanatory step; do not force a paper screenshot into every post.
- Preserve relevant uncertainty. Do not turn an association, mechanism, deficiency effect, or conditional finding into a universal outcome.
- Do not use publication screenshots, author names, journal branding, or a bibliography as visual proof by themselves.
- Every cited source must have been read and admitted by the global expertise owner, and each on-screen claim must be traceable to what that source actually supports.
- A review or position paper may summarize a field but does not automatically validate every downstream prescription.
- Use one decisive emphasized phrase instead of styling several unrelated claims as equally important.

### 3. Explanation or mechanism

Explain why the distinction matters to the selected training decision.

```text
[plain-language mechanism or relationship]
[what changes]
[why that matters here]
```

- Define a necessary technical term in ordinary U.S. English before relying on it.
- Keep mechanism and outcome distinct. A physiological role does not by itself prove that more intake, more activation, or a specific intervention improves results.
- Distinguish normal function, deficiency correction, and enhancement above adequacy.
- Include a relevant condition or limitation when omitting it would materially overstate the evidence.
- Do not stack a list of biological processes merely to create scientific weight.

### 4. Examples, decision factors, or dosage

Make the explanation concrete through a small number of relevant examples or observable inputs.

```text
[example or decision factor]
[its relevant function or condition]
[what the viewer may conclude—and what they may not]
```

- Select examples because they clarify the decision, not because they allow a longer scientific list.
- Quantify loads, repetitions, sets, rest, nutrients, or frequency only when the exact values and applicable situation are supported by admitted expertise.
- Avoid unsupported `for most people`, `for most exercises`, and one-size-fits-all prescriptions.
- State deficiency-dependent or adequacy-dependent effects explicitly when required.
- Distinguish an example from a recommendation and a normal physiological role from a performance claim.

### 5. Scope, application, or qualification

Reconnect the examples to the broader decision without expanding beyond the evidence.

```text
[what the example demonstrates]
[other relevant factors or boundary]
[whole-decision principle]
```

- Widen the scope only when the added factors are individually relevant and supported.
- Do not turn a long list into an implication that every viewer needs supplementation, testing, or correction.
- Avoid vague whole-body language when a more precise condition can be stated.
- Use first person only when the experience belongs to an identified real source that the project may truthfully represent.
- Do not write `How I use it`, `what worked for me`, or similar lived-experience language in the brand's voice without verified experience.

### 6. Final takeaway

Close with the decision the viewer should carry into training.

```text
[what is insufficient or commonly overemphasized]
[what to consider or control instead]
[short bounded action principle]
```

- Resolve the opening without restating every slide.
- Prefer an actionable principle over a slogan.
- Keep the conclusion no broader than the evidence already presented.
- A food, exercise, or training-action list must follow from the body rather than appear as generic wellness advice.
- The takeaway is not automatically a CTA.

## 3. Writing and emphasis

### Voice

- Use idiomatic U.S. English and direct `you` when appropriate.
- Sound informed, controlled, practical, and physically grounded rather than academic, cute, meme-like, or performatively authoritative.
- Explain technical material without talking down to the viewer.
- Use aggression only for training effort; use restraint for loading, fatigue, progression, and evidence claims.
- Avoid empty `optimize`, `unlock`, `secret`, `hack`, `best ever`, and unsupported `science says` language.
- Do not use the word `CODE` as a hacker or software metaphor.
- Run a separate grammar and idiomatic-English pass. Reference errors are not part of the format.

### Reading density

The carousel gives the viewer control over reading pace, but that does not justify unreadable paragraphs.

- Give each slide one main explanatory job.
- Use a concise headline and only the body text required to understand that job.
- Break a complex explanation into another slide rather than shrinking type or stacking loosely related claims.
- Keep a clear reading path from headline to supported claim to practical implication.
- Use bullets only for genuinely parallel examples or actions.
- If a citation block competes with the claim it supports, move full source detail to the caption while retaining enough identification for traceability.
- Keep terminology stable across the sequence.

### Semantic emphasis

The references repeatedly separate language through accent color and underlining. Preserve the hierarchy, not the exact palette.

- Mark the hook contrast, decisive evidence-backed statement, and practical consequence as different semantic roles only when each distinction helps reading.
- Do not emphasize every technical noun or whole sentence.
- Use emphasis consistently inside one post; the same treatment should not mean `evidence` on one slide and `warning` on another.
- Exact color, underline, shadow, and typography values belong to the native project and imagery guidance.

### Character relationship

The references show that a character may appear on attention, explanation, example, or scope slides and may be absent from evidence-heavy or final slides. Character presence is not a required copy slot.

- Do not put scientific claims in a character's mouth to make them feel more trustworthy.
- Do not invent dialogue, personal results, credentials, or training history for a visual character.
- Keep all necessary meaning in the approved copy; the visual figure must not become a second factual owner.
- Character or figure selection, consistency, styling, and semantic pose belong to imagery guidance and the native project, not this language grammar.

## 4. CTA, title, and caption

Both reference posts end with a takeaway and show no dedicated CTA slide.

- Default to no dedicated CTA slide.
- When appropriate, place one relevant action in the caption, such as saving the explanation or following for another bounded training decision.
- Provide a separate concise post title with every final copy. The title is not part of the caption.
- Compose the caption with one concise description, then a brief evidence or scope note when the claim requires it, followed by a small set of relevant hashtags.
- Name only sources actually admitted by the global expertise owner. Do not fabricate citations or imply that one paper settles a broader question.
- Preserve claim-to-source traceability. A citation list must not be broader or narrower than the claims it is meant to support.
- Use the caption to preserve an important limitation that cannot fit naturally on a slide, not to repair an unsupported body claim after publication.
- Do not introduce product promotion during the current no-product prelaunch phase.
