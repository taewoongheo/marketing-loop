# Copywriting: denzel

## 1. Scope and evidence

This file owns how a selected env message is expressed in the `denzel` slideshow: slide roles, progression, wording, tone, emphasis, image–copy relationship, product reveal, CTA, and caption.

- Message meaning and claim limits come from the selected immutable message version.
- Product truth comes from `context/product.md`.
- Approved content inputs come from `materials.md`.
- The renderer template owns slide count, slots, geometry, typography, image crop, and editable properties.
- `references/post-01/` through `references/post-05/` contain five user-designated posts in slide order. The first four have seven slides; `post-05` has eight.
- All five posts use one hook slide, a numbered body series, and the same low-information closing treatment. The body series contains five or six slides; hook wording, body-title syntax, paragraph shape, and topic progression vary.
- Reference slide counts describe the source evidence, not the renderer template's slot contract.
- Rules shared by all five posts are stable for the current format analysis. Less consistent patterns are optional variations, not requirements.
- References are evidence of technique only. Never copy their topic, wording, examples, signature, or distinctive subjects.

## 2. Slide flow

### Hook slide

Open with a useful or counterintuitive topic statement, then qualify it with the narrator's personal relationship to that topic. The qualifier makes the perspective feel lived-in and unfinished rather than preached from authority.

```text
[useful outcome, avoided consequence, or overlooked idea]
[from/as someone who is learning, used to believe, or used to do something relevant]
```

- Make the main topic understandable without the qualifier.
- Use the qualifier to establish relevance, not expertise or a guaranteed result.
- The narrator may be correcting a former mistake or still learning a better approach; neither form is mandatory.
- Do not put `env` in the hook unless product recognition is necessary to understand the message.
- Promise a useful perspective or route, never guaranteed focus, grades, or productivity.

### Body slides

Use the numbered body slides to develop distinct facets under one hook promise. For env, every facet must support the selected message rather than drift into generic focus advice.

1. The first body slide makes the broad hook concrete through one important behavior, influence, or principle.
2. The remaining body slides add distinct, parallel facets of the same topic.
3. Do not invent a required escalation or final resolution: the references support both reflective progression and a flat list of equally weighted points.

Each body slide contains:

```text
[N]. [concise point title]

[plain correction or recognizable pattern].
[brief reason, contrast, or consequence].

[optional conclusion or short callout].
```

The title may be an imperative, noun phrase, or gerund phrase. The explanation may end inside one paragraph or use a separate conclusion or callout; do not force all slides into the same paragraph shape. The number of body slides follows the renderer template, and each slide must advance the message instead of rephrasing an earlier slide.

### CTA slide

After the body slides complete the advice, use the renderer template's dedicated CTA slide to connect the selected message to `env`. Its headline makes the transition from principle to implementation, its supporting copy explains the one relevant product capability, and its product image provides visual context. Add no unrelated advice or broad feature list here.

## 3. Writing

### Reusable sentence patterns

Use these as flexible structures, not fixed wording:

```text
[familiar thing] may seem [small or unimportant], but [larger effect or consequence].
```

```text
[common assumption or old rule].
[correction, contrast, or overlooked reason].
[reader-centered conclusion].
```

```text
[short list of concrete actions, influences, or obstacles].
[the shared effect they have].
[what the reader should notice or do differently].
```

Use only the parts needed by a slide. The common rhythm is point title followed by explanation; a separate conclusion is optional. Vary sentence openings and paragraph length so the copy does not feel mechanical.

### Voice, rhythm, and emphasis

- Use conversational U.S. English, direct `you`, and mostly lowercase styling when compatible with the template.
- Use `I` briefly when a personal admission earns trust; do not make the post primarily about the narrator.
- Sound calm, corrective, and humane rather than motivational, clinical, or superior.
- Prefer observable actions and ordinary words over abstract strategy language.
- Use contrast to clarify the reframe: old rule versus replacement rule, appearance versus reality, or pressure versus a sustainable standard.
- Keep one main claim per sentence and one argument per slide.
- Use short concrete lists only when every item supports the same point.
- Underline a small number of phrases that carry the decisive correction, consequence, or conclusion. The exact count follows the copy and available text marks rather than a fixed quota.
- Let the headline carry the claim and the remaining copy explain it. End on the strongest conclusion without summarizing it again.
- Avoid scolding, hustle language, moral judgment, diagnoses, and universal psychological claims.

## 4. Image–copy relationship

- Use each image slot supplied by the template to support the point made on that slide; image count and placement remain template-owned.
- Let the copy make the argument and the image supply mood, familiarity, visual texture, or a human-scale scene.
- Prefer casual, tactile, or slightly imperfect imagery over polished productivity stock imagery.
- The image may show an everyday situation, use an abstract or aesthetic close-up, echo the point metaphorically, or provide quiet visual relief.
- Do not describe what the image already makes obvious.
- Do not use reference screenshots as production assets or reproduce their distinctive subjects.
- On the dedicated CTA slide, let the fixed product image establish the app context; the copy should explain why the shown capability matters rather than narrate visible interface details.

## 5. env and caption

None of the five references contains a product reveal or CTA. The renderer template's dedicated CTA slide is therefore an env-specific adaptation, not a reference-derived rule.

- Keep the slideshow centered on the selected belief shift, not on env's feature set.
- Keep the hook and body slides app-independent. Introduce `env` on the dedicated CTA slide only after the old pattern and replacement principle are understood.
- Present env as the practical implementation of the established principle: a prepared focus environment that moves recurring setup choices out of the starting moment.
- Mention only the capability needed by that slide; never turn it into a feature list.
- Explain what changed in the setup rather than claiming env is universally superior.
- Resolve on the user's transition into the intended work, not on opening or configuring the app.
- Use one direct CTA related to preparing or starting the relevant focus environment; never promise generic productivity outcomes.
- The caption should name or extend the main reframe without recapping every slide or repeating the full product pitch.
- Use only a small set of relevant hashtags; hashtags are not performance evidence.
