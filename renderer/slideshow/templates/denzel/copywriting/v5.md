# Copywriting: denzel v5

## 1. Scope and evidence

This file owns how a selected env message is expressed in the `denzel` slideshow through language: slide-copy roles, progression, wording, voice, emphasis, product reveal, CTA, title, and caption.

- Message meaning and claim limits come from the selected message version.
- Product truth comes from `context/product.md`.
- Approved content inputs come from `materials.md`.
- The renderer template owns slide count, slots, geometry, typography, image crop, and editable properties.
- `imagery/v1.md` separately owns visual tone, image–copy coordination, prompt adaptation, generation settings, and exclusions. Copy should remain compatible with that interface without restating its rules here.
- `references/post-01/` through `references/post-05/` contain five user-designated posts in slide order. The first four have seven slides; `post-05` has eight.
- All five posts use one hook slide, a numbered body series, and the same low-information closing treatment. The body series contains five or six slides; hook wording, body-title syntax, paragraph shape, and topic progression vary.
- Reference slide counts describe the source evidence, not the renderer template's slot contract.
- Rules shared by all five posts are stable for the current format analysis. Less consistent patterns are optional variations, not requirements.
- References are evidence of technique only. Never copy their topic, wording, examples, signature, or distinctive subjects.

## 2. Slide flow

### Hook slide

Open with a useful or counterintuitive topic statement, then qualify it with the narrator's personal relationship to that topic. The qualifier makes the perspective feel lived-in and unfinished rather than preached from authority.

```text
[useful outcome, avoided consequence, or overlooked idea]
[from/as someone who is learning, used to believe, or used to do something relevant]
```

- Make the main topic understandable without the qualifier.
- Use the qualifier to establish relevance, not expertise or a guaranteed result.
- Prefer `as someone who...` when the narrator is giving advice from a shared experience or correcting a former belief. Use `from someone who...` only when framing the narrator as the source adds useful meaning; do not choose it mechanically.
- The narrator may be correcting a former mistake or still learning a better approach; neither form is mandatory.
- Do not put `env` in the hook unless product recognition is necessary to understand the message.
- Promise a useful perspective or route, never guaranteed focus, grades, or productivity.
- Across separate contents, vary the hook architecture as part of the copywriting execution. Do not default to a numbered `N ways` promise merely because the body slides are numbered; rotate among useful outcome statements, overlooked-problem statements, contrasts, questions, or personal admissions when they fit the selected message. Reuse a numbered-promise structure only when the confirmed experiment intentionally holds it constant.
- Match the hook grammar to what the numbered slides actually deliver. Use a concise `how to...`, `things to try...`, or equivalent practical-route hook when the body supplies methods; use an explanatory or counterintuitive statement only when the body actually explains that claim. A non-numbered hook may introduce a numbered body, but it must not promise a cause, comparison, or conclusion that the body does not resolve.
- Lead with the reader-facing stake, not the operational mechanism. A hook about rebuilding a setup, changing settings, or using a system must make clear why that matters to the target user—for example, delaying the first task action, losing intended study time, or making the start feel harder. Apply a strict relevance test: if a reader could reasonably think `what does this have to do with me?`, rewrite the hook around the recognizable consequence before naming the mechanism. Never manufacture unsupported losses involving money, intelligence, grades, or opportunity merely to raise the stakes.

### Body slides

Use the numbered body slides to develop distinct facets under one hook promise. For env, every facet must support the selected message rather than drift into generic focus advice. When the hook promises a numbered set of methods, count the dedicated CTA slide as the final method: the body slides carry methods `1` through `N-1`, and the CTA carries method `N`. Do not promise only the body-slide count and then add env as an uncounted extra method.

Before drafting any numbered copy, make an internal slide-to-material map that assigns exactly one distinct approved method from `materials.md` to every numbered slide, including the CTA. Do not draft until the map covers every numbered slide, and reject any draft whose numbered point lacks that one-to-one mapping or repeats a method within the slideshow. Problem scenes, message logic, and belief shifts may explain why a method matters, but they must not be counted as methods. The CTA method must be one that `env` can truthfully implement through the product capability named on that slide.

1. The first body slide makes the broad hook concrete through one important behavior, influence, or principle.
2. The remaining body slides add distinct, parallel facets of the same topic.
3. Do not invent a required escalation or final resolution: the references support both reflective progression and a flat list of equally weighted points.

Each body slide contains:

```text
[N]. [concise point title]

[plain correction or recognizable pattern].
[brief reason, contrast, or consequence].

[optional conclusion or short callout].
```

The title may be an imperative, noun phrase, or gerund phrase. The explanation may end inside one paragraph or use a separate conclusion or callout; do not force all slides into the same paragraph shape. The number of body slides follows the renderer template, and each slide must advance the message instead of rephrasing an earlier slide.

### CTA slide

Use the renderer template's dedicated CTA slide to complete the advice by expressing the final method through `env`, rather than appending a separate product pitch after the promised set is complete. When the hook is numbered, continue that numbering on the CTA. Its headline and opening copy must first state the selected message's core reframe as useful focus advice; do not make app introduction the slide's main claim. Then connect that advice to the one relevant product capability that implements it. On first mention, write `"env" app` so an unfamiliar reader can recognize that `env` is the app name. Add no unrelated advice or broad feature list.

Keep the CTA's reading length and information density comparable to the other numbered slides. The need to include a message reframe and product bridge does not justify a longer closing slide; if it runs long, remove repeated setup explanation and secondary product detail rather than compressing more copy into the slot.

Across separate contents using the same message, vary the CTA's headline, reframe wording, sentence structure, product bridge, and direct action so the copywriting execution is genuinely different. Do not reuse a previous CTA verbatim or preserve its surface phrasing merely because the semantic message is fixed, unless the confirmed hypothesis plan explicitly holds that CTA wording constant as a control.

## 3. Writing

### Reusable sentence patterns

Use these as flexible structures, not fixed wording:

```text
[familiar thing] may seem [small or unimportant], but [larger effect or consequence].
```

```text
[common assumption or old rule].
[correction, contrast, or overlooked reason].
[reader-centered conclusion].
```

```text
[short list of concrete actions, influences, or obstacles].
[the shared effect they have].
[what the reader should notice or do differently].
```

Use only the parts needed by a slide. The common rhythm is point title followed by explanation; a separate conclusion is optional. Vary sentence openings and paragraph length so the copy does not feel mechanical.

### Voice, rhythm, and emphasis

- Use conversational U.S. English, direct `you`, and mostly lowercase styling when compatible with the template.
- Before showing a draft, run a separate idiomatic U.S. English pass over every line. Replace literal or merely grammatical phrasing with the collocation a native speaker would normally use—for example, prefer `start studying`, `make it easier to get started`, `decide how to start again`, and `set everything up again` over compressed constructions that sound translated.
- Keep situation nouns consistent within one content. Do not switch among `study`, `work`, `task`, or similar frames unless the shift is intentional and semantically necessary; revise the surrounding sentence rather than making an isolated word replacement that sounds unnatural.
- Use `I` briefly when a personal admission earns trust; do not make the post primarily about the narrator.
- Sound calm, corrective, and humane rather than motivational, clinical, or superior.
- Prefer observable actions and ordinary words over abstract strategy language.
- Use contrast to clarify the reframe: old rule versus replacement rule, appearance versus reality, or pressure versus a sustainable standard.
- Keep one main claim per sentence and one argument per slide.
- Use short concrete lists only when every item supports the same point.
- Underline a small number of phrases that carry the decisive correction, consequence, or conclusion. The exact count follows the copy and available text marks rather than a fixed quota.
- Let the headline carry the claim and the remaining copy explain it. End on the strongest conclusion without summarizing it again.
- Avoid scolding, hustle language, moral judgment, diagnoses, and universal psychological claims.

## 4. env and caption

None of the five references contains a product reveal or CTA. The renderer template's dedicated CTA slide is therefore an env-specific adaptation, not a reference-derived rule.

- Keep the slideshow centered on the selected belief shift, not on env's feature set.
- Keep the hook and body slides app-independent. Introduce `env` on the dedicated CTA slide only after the old pattern and replacement principle are understood.
- Present env as the practical implementation of the established principle: a prepared focus environment that moves recurring setup choices out of the starting moment.
- Mention only the capability needed by that slide; never turn it into a feature list.
- Explain what changed in the setup rather than claiming env is universally superior.
- Resolve on the user's transition into the intended work, not on opening or configuring the app.
- Use one direct CTA related to preparing or starting the relevant focus environment; never promise generic productivity outcomes.
- Provide a separate concise post title with every final copy. The title names the post for publication and is not part of the caption.
- Compose the caption as one short description sentence followed by a small set of relevant hashtags. The description should name or extend the main reframe without recapping the slides or repeating the product pitch. Add a second sentence only when essential context would otherwise be missing; do not use multiple sentences merely to summarize the post.
- Hashtags are part of the caption but are not performance evidence.
