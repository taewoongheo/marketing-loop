# Imagery: denzel v1

## 1. Scope, inputs, and outputs

This file owns the reusable rules that translate approved slide meaning into imagery for the `denzel` slideshow: semantic visualization, art direction, within-image composition, runtime request constraints, generation settings, and selection policy.

- The selected message version owns persuasion strategy and claim limits.
- The selected copywriting version owns the actual hook, body, CTA, title, caption, and all language rules.
- `materials.md` owns approved content inputs, not visual instructions.
- `template.json` owns the live slide count and order, image-slot presence, slot geometry, crop, editable properties, and fixed product image.
- `references/` owns the raw reference screenshots. This file owns only the reusable visual interpretation derived from them.
- The editable project owns the exact approved copy and resulting image bytes used for one content.

At generation time, the assistant reads the approved project copy, live image slot, and this imagery version, then constructs the provider request as a transient execution value. Do not persist that request as a separate artifact. Do not store post-specific copy, a duplicate slide count, final images, or object- and topic-specific default descriptions in this file. Concrete examples may explain a rule, but they are nonbinding and must not become default request content.

## 2. Semantic translation

For each live image slot whose source is editable, derive one visual scene from the approved meaning of that slide.

- Let the copy make the argument. Use the image as nonverbal support through a recognizable situation, mood, texture, or human-scale action.
- Extract the reader-facing situation, consequence, or action rather than converting the slide sentence word for word.
- Show one dominant idea per image. Do not combine every sentence or method from the slide into one scene.
- Keep all necessary argumentative meaning in the copy; the image must not introduce an unsupported claim or a second method.
- **Hook:** visualize the recognizable situation, friction, or consequence that makes the topic personally relevant. Favor the lived moment over an abstract mechanism.
- **Method:** visualize one concrete action or ordinary situation that supports that method. The treatment may be literal, lightly metaphorical, or atmospheric.
- **CTA:** preserve the template’s fixed product image when its source is not editable. Do not generate a replacement merely to make every slide visually uniform.

Use semantic roles found in the live project; do not assume a fixed number of generated slides.

## 3. Art direction

- Use believable, casual point-and-shoot photography with natural available light or a subtle direct-flash feel.
- Favor tactile, slightly grainy, lived-in imagery over polished stock photography or glossy advertising.
- Keep settings, objects, clothing, and gestures ordinary rather than aspirational or luxury-coded.
- Prefer candid framing, partial faces, and natural body posture when they communicate the moment more credibly than a posed portrait.
- Allow modest imperfection and visual texture.
- Keep generated slides within one photographic family through compatible lighting, realism, grain, and color treatment. Do not force the same person or location unless the approved content requires continuity.
- Represent the target audience without turning ethnicity, gender, appearance, or institutional identity into the point of the image.

## 4. Within-image composition

- Read the live slot’s aspect ratio and crop behavior; do not restate or own its geometry here.
- Compose for the final crop, with the focal subject or action inside a safe central region.
- Keep the dominant action immediately legible and avoid placing essential details at the edges.
- Use one plausible environment and only the visual elements needed to make the scene recognizable.

## 5. Runtime request constraints

Construct one provider request from the approved semantic meaning, art direction, within-image composition, and the following exclusions. Use a plain semantic description instead of copying the approved slide wording when possible.

Encode these as request constraints:

- no readable captions, labels, logos, watermarks, or unapproved brand marks;
- no fake `env` interface or unapproved product UI;
- no visible interface content unless it is truthful, necessary, and approved;
- no visual claim that exceeds product truth or the selected message’s evidence limits;
- no copied reference subject, composition, signature, or distinctive expression;
- no collage, split screen, before/after layout, or multiple competing scenes unless explicitly required by the approved content and live template;
- no obvious anatomy defect, impossible object, or malformed visual element.

These exclusions guide the provider request. They do not authorize subjective selection or another paid generation after a successful image has been returned.

## 6. Generation profile and selection policy

- Provider: OpenAI Images API
- Model: `gpt-image-2`
- Quality: `medium`
- Successful results per eligible slot: exactly `1`
- Subjective selection: none
- Subjective regeneration: none
- Output format: PNG
- Output dimensions: choose one model-supported size that closely matches the live slot ratio
- Maximum technical retries after the initial request: `3`
- Maximum total attempts per eligible slot: `4`

A technical failure means that no usable image payload was returned because of a transport or API error, empty response, undecodable payload, or invalid output format or dimensions. Retry only such a failure and stop after at most three retries. The first technically usable image ends the attempt sequence and is used directly even when its aesthetics or adherence to non-safety request details are imperfect. Never spend another request merely because a successful image is less attractive than expected.

## 7. v1 rationale

The initial GPT Image 2 medium test produced one usable result for each editable Denzel image slot. v1 separates the reusable visual behavior discovered in that test from copywriting. It preserves the established casual, tactile image–copy relationship while making approved copy and the live template explicit runtime inputs rather than duplicating either one here.
