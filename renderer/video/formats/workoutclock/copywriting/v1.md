# Copywriting: workoutclock v1

## 1. Scope and evidence

This file owns how a selected LIFT CODE message is expressed through language in the `workoutclock` video format: on-screen text roles, progression, wording, rhythm, spoken-text use, CTA, title, and caption.

- Message meaning and claim limits come from the selected message version.
- Product truth comes from `context/product.md`.
- Reusable strength-training knowledge and provenance come from `context/expertise.md`.
- Each native video project owns exact geometry, typography, clip timing, motion, crop, assets, and audio levels.
- `renderer/video/formats/workoutclock/references/workoutclock1/` and `workoutclock2/` contain two user-designated source videos.
- Both references are silent clips of approximately six seconds with no hard cuts. They sustain several simultaneous animated exercise demonstrations, compact set-and-rep labels, and adjacent muscle-target imagery.
- `workoutclock1` relies on exercise demonstrations and prescription labels without a sentence-level hook. `workoutclock2` adds one short sentence presented as rapidly replacing single-word beats.
- The captured platform search bar, navigation controls, mute control, and black surrounding area are source-viewer chrome, not format grammar.
- The references establish execution and language technique only. Never copy their exercise selection, wording, prescription, anatomical artwork, branding, or distinctive visual assets.

Domain knowledge is global rather than workoutclock-specific. This format decides how to compress approved expertise into its language channels; it does not own exercise recommendations or set-and-rep prescriptions. The references' repeated `3×12` label is structural evidence of a compact prescription marker, not evidence that `3×12` is appropriate for future content.

## 2. Copy flow

### Hook channel

Choose one of two reference-supported approaches according to the selected message and content:

1. **Sequential hook:** express one short training statement through rapidly replacing word or short-phrase beats while the exercise board remains useful without it.
2. **Label-only opening:** omit a sentence-level hook when the exercise selection, target, and prescription are immediately legible on their own.

For a sequential hook:

```text
[recognizable training goal or decision]
[constraint, selection principle, or useful distinction]
```

- The complete statement must remain understandable when read in order.
- Use only as many beats as the sentence needs; do not pad the sequence to fill the clip.
- Prefer concrete training language over suspense, motivational filler, or exaggerated promises.
- Do not repeat the same complete sentence elsewhere on screen.
- The hook may identify what the exercise group is for, how the options differ, or what constraint organizes them. It must not imply that a short routine guarantees strength or hypertrophy.

### Exercise and prescription labels

Use compact labels that let the viewer identify and compare the displayed training options without explanatory paragraphs.

Possible roles are:

```text
[exercise name or variation]
[target or selection condition]
[sets] × [reps or rep range]
```

- Include only the labels needed to make the board usable.
- Keep terminology parallel across simultaneous exercise demonstrations.
- Use a set-and-rep label only when the exact prescription is supported by admitted expertise and the content's stated situation. Do not inherit `3×12` from the references by default.
- If exercise names are omitted, the demonstration and approved visual execution must still make the distinction legible.
- Do not place form coaching, mechanism explanations, and programming caveats into the same compact label system. Choose a different format when the useful message requires that density.

### Spoken text

The references contain no audio. Default `spoken_text` to an empty channel.

Use spoken text only when the approved message cannot remain intelligible through the compact on-screen system. Spoken text must add necessary explanation rather than narrate every visible label. Adding it is an adaptation, not a reference-derived requirement.

## 3. Writing

### Voice and rhythm

- Use idiomatic U.S. English.
- Sound direct, controlled, physically grounded, and useful rather than loud, clinical, or ornamental.
- Prefer concrete nouns and verbs such as press, curl, row, load, reps, range, angle, and control.
- Keep each sequential beat immediately legible; avoid articles or filler when removing them preserves natural meaning.
- Keep exercise and prescription labels grammatically parallel.
- Use numerals for compact training prescriptions.
- Avoid `secret`, `hack`, `best ever`, guaranteed-result language, and unsupported `science says` authority.
- Do not use the word `CODE` as a hacker or software metaphor.
- Run an idiomatic U.S.-English pass before showing final copy.

### Information limit

This format is for immediate recognition and comparison, not layered explanation.

- One short hook idea is the maximum sentence-level argument.
- The remaining language identifies exercises, targets, conditions, or prescriptions.
- If the audience must understand why a recommendation works, where an exception applies, or how several constraints interact, select another format rather than shrinking the explanation into unreadable text.

## 4. CTA, title, and caption

The references show no dedicated CTA. Default to no on-screen CTA so the useful exercise board can loop without a separate closing scene.

- When an action is appropriate, place it in the caption or use one very short on-screen action only if it does not displace required training information.
- Provide a separate concise post title with every final copy. The title is not part of the caption.
- Compose the caption as one short description sentence followed by a small set of relevant hashtags.
- Let the caption state the applicable situation or selection principle that the compact board cannot fully express.
- Do not use the caption to introduce unsupported exercise prescriptions, guaranteed outcomes, or product promotion during the current no-product prelaunch phase.
